# What is Wave Energy 
Ocean waves carry enormous amounts of energy. Understanding where that energy comes from — and how we might capture it — is the first step to appreciating wave energy converters. 

## Where do Waves Get Their Energy? 
Most ocean waves are created by wind. As wind blows across the water surface, friction between the moving air and the water transfers energy into the water, creating ripples that grow into waves. The longer the wind blows, and the larger the area of ocean it acts on (<em>the fetch</em>), the bigger and more energetic the waves become. 

Once a wave is formed, it can travel thousands of kilometers across open ocean with very little energy loss. The wave is not moving water from place to place; it is moving energy. Individual water particles trace roughly circular paths as the wave passes, returning nearly to where they started.

<strong>Key idea:</strong> A wave is a pattern of energy moving through the water, not a bulk movement of water itself.

## Describing a Wave 
Two measurements describe the shape of a regular ocean wave: 
- <strong>Wave height (H)</strong> — the vertical distance from trough to crest. 
- <strong>Wave period (T)</strong> — the time, in seconds, between two successive crests passing the same point. 

From the period we can calculate the <em>wave frequency</em>: how many complete oscillations occur each second (measured in hertz, Hz). A 10-second period wave has a frequency of 0.1 Hz. 

## How Much Energy Does a Wave Carry? 
The power per unit width of wave crest (often called the <em>wave power density</em>) depends on both wave height and wave period: 

<code class="equation">P/width ≈ (ρ · g² / 32π) · H² · T</code> 

where <em>ρ</em> is the density of seawater (~1025 kg/m³), <em>g</em> is gravitational acceleration (~9.81 m/s²), <em>H</em> is wave height in metres, and <em>T</em> is wave period in seconds. The important takeaway is that power grows quickly with wave height (it scales as H²) and also increases with period. 

Energetic ocean locations — off the western coasts of Europe and North America, for instance — can see average wave power densities of 30–80 kilowatts per meter of wave crest. 

## Why Is Wave Energy Interesting? 
Wave energy has several attractive features as a renewable resource: 
- It is available around the clock, unlike solar power. 
- It is more predictable than wind, because swell can be forecast days in advance. 
- The energy density is high compared with wind at the same area. 

The challenge is converting that energy reliably and cost-effectively. That is exactly what a wave energy converter tries to do — and what this simulator helps you explore.
